Your Brain on Media

You probably know that junk food is bad for your body. But can you guess which type of media is actually harmful for your brain — and which one is the equivalent of a home-cooked meal?

↓ Scroll to explore the menu

Think of your brain like your stomach. A little junk food is fine — it's part of life. But a diet of only junk food will make you feel slow and tired over time. The same is true for your mind.

Below, you will find seven types of media described as food. Click each card to discover the science, step by step. The key message? It's all about balance: no single type of media is completely good or completely bad.

Junk Food
📱 TikTok & Short-Form Videos
⚠️ High risk if overused ⚡ Very high stimulation
"Impossible to stop at just one."
When you scroll through TikTok, your brain gets a small reward every few seconds. Over time, it learns to expect fast, exciting content. This makes slower activities such as reading or concentrating in class feel much harder and more boring. A 2025 meta-analysis of dozens of studies found that heavy short-form use is linked to shorter attention spans and lower academic performance.
🍽️ Suggested daily serving
Treat it like a snack, not a meal. Around 30 minutes a day is a healthier limit. If you notice it's hard to concentrate after scrolling, that's your brain telling you it needs a break.

Pizza
▶️ Long YouTube Videos
👍 Better than short clips 🍕 Toppings matter
"Better than nuggets. But toppings still matter."
Longer videos require you to pay attention for more than a few seconds, which is already better for your brain than short clips. A field experiment showed that increasing long-form viewing did not cause the attention problems associated with short-form content. However, watching passively is still fairly easy for the brain, and the content matters: a documentary trains your thinking more than a prank video.
🍽️ Suggested daily serving
Fine in moderation. When you can, choose content that teaches you something. Your brain gets more from an explanation than from entertainment alone.

Protein Bar
💬 Comics & Graphic Novels
✅ Underrated brain food 🧩 Builds inference skills
"More nutritious than people think."
When you read a comic or graphic novel, your brain processes both text and images at the same time. Research shows this activates the same cognitive processes as advanced text comprehension. You also have to "fill in the gaps" between panels, which is excellent practice for inference and critical thinking. Comics are especially helpful for language learners.
🍽️ Suggested daily serving
Enjoy freely! Comics are a great gateway to reading — they build vocabulary, visual literacy, and comprehension skills at the same time. Not just for children.

🎵 Listening to Music
🎯 Context-dependent 😊 Powerful mood booster
"Can make everything better. Can also backfire."
Music is a surprisingly powerful tool for the brain. Research from Johns Hopkins shows it can improve mood, reduce anxiety, and boost focus and memory. However, the type of music matters greatly. Music without lyrics, such as classical or lo-fi, helps you concentrate. Music with lyrics competes directly with the language centre of your brain, which can cause distraction while reading or writing.
🍽️ Suggested daily serving
Instrumental music while studying = excellent. Lyrics while writing = risky. Save your favourite songs for commuting, exercise, or breaks.

Smoothie
🎙️ Podcasts
💪 Builds critical thinking 🗣️ Great for vocabulary
"Packed with good stuff. Goes down easily."
Listening to a podcast activates the social part of your brain — it feels like being part of a real conversation. Stanford University research found that podcast listening improves critical thinking skills. For English learners specifically, podcasts are excellent: you hear natural, spoken language in real contexts, which is one of the most effective ways to build vocabulary and listening comprehension.
🍽️ Suggested daily serving
Ideal during commutes, exercise, or household tasks. Choose topics you are genuinely curious about — your brain engages much more when you care about the content.

Meal Delivery
🎧 Audiobooks
🌟 Almost identical to reading ♿ Great for accessibility
"Same nutrition. No preparation required."
Researchers at UC Berkeley scanned people's brains while they both read and listened to the same stories. The result surprised them: the brain activated almost identical areas in both cases. Audiobooks also build your ability to focus over time. Following a long narrative for hours trains the brain to sustain attention. They are especially valuable for people with dyslexia or visual difficulties.
🍽️ Suggested daily serving
A genuinely great option, especially when commuting or doing something with your hands. Do not feel guilty for choosing an audiobook over a physical book, because your brain gets most of the same benefits.

Home-Cooked Meal
📖 Reading a Book
🏆 Maximum brain nutrition ⏱️ Requires patience
"Takes effort. Worth every bite."
Reading a book, especially fiction, produces some of the most powerful effects on the brain of any media. Brain scans show that reading a novel increases connectivity between brain areas linked to language, sensation, and empathy, and these effects last for several days after you finish. One 14-year study found that reading at least once a week reduces the risk of cognitive decline by 46%.
🍽️ Suggested daily serving
Even 20–30 minutes a day produces measurable benefits. It feels difficult at first, like cooking when you're used to takeaway, but it gets easier and the long-term rewards are remarkable.
