Is, Are, Do, Does

The verb must agree with the subject — not the nearest noun. Ignore phrases beginning with of, as well as, or along with. Then ask: singular or plural?
Special cases: -ics words (economics, mathematics) are singular. Gerund phrases ("Playing chess") count as one subject. With either/or and neither/nor: if one element is plural, the verb is plural.

Who or Whom?

Who is a subject pronoun — it does the action (like he/she). Whom is an object pronoun — something is done to it (like him/her).
The same rules apply to whoever and whomever.
🔑 The he/him test: rewrite the sentence and substitute he or him. If he sounds right → use who. If him sounds right → use whom.

Comma or No Comma?

Key rules to keep in mind: use a comma before but / and / or only when joining two independent clauses. Use a comma after introductory phrases (usually 3+ words). Use commas around non-essential elements. Adverbs like however need commas when they interrupt — but not when they're essential to the clause's meaning.

The Ellipsis

An ellipsis (…) is three dots used to indicate something has been left out — or left unsaid. It has five distinct purposes: pause in speech, creating suspense, expecting an answer, unfinished thought, and excitement after a pause. The Em-Dash

The em-dash (—) is the length of the letter M and has no spaces before or after it. It has five distinct purposes: adding emphasis, break in thought, explaining or defining, trailing off (unfinished sentence), and separating two clauses. Each card below shows a sentence with an em-dash already in place — identify what it is doing.

The en-dash (–) simply means "through" and is used for ranges: dates, page numbers, scores. September 9–November 11 · pages 11–99. No spaces around it either.

Colon or Semicolon?

The colon (:) introduces something — a list, an explanation, a quotation, or a definition. Think of it as an equals sign: what follows equals or explains what came before.
The semicolon (;) connects two related independent clauses (stronger than a comma, weaker than a full stop). It is also used before transitional phrases like however, therefore, namely — with a comma after the transitional word but not before it.
🐉

Hyphen or No Hyphen?

Hyphens connect parts of compound adjectives. The key rules: compound adjectives before a noun → hyphenate. After a noun → usually no hyphen. -ly adverb + adjective → never hyphenate. Age terms → hyphenate in both positions. Participle constructions (ending in -ing/-ed) → hyphenate before the noun only.

Dangling & Misplaced Modifiers

A participle phrase (opening with a verb ending in -ing or -ed) must modify the subject of the main clause. If it ends up attached to the wrong noun — or to nothing at all — it is dangling. Limiting modifiers (almost, nearly, only, just) must sit directly before the word they modify. One position change can completely flip the meaning of a sentence.
🐉
📍

Dangling Prepositions

Ending a sentence with a preposition is widely accepted in modern English when doing so is natural and graceful. There is, however, one absolute rule with no exceptions: never end a sentence with at. If removing 'at' leaves a perfectly complete sentence, it was never needed.
🐉
👁️

Squinting Modifiers

A squinting modifier is an adverb placed so it could modify either the words before it or the words after it — leaving the reader uncertain which was intended. The fix is always the same: move the adverb so it sits directly next to the word it actually describes.

Independent or Dependent?

An independent clause has a subject and verb and can stand alone as a complete sentence. A dependent clause also has a subject and verb, but begins with a subordinating word (when, because, although, if...) that makes it incomplete — it needs an independent clause to finish the thought. These concepts underpin everything else in this series: which/that, subject-verb agreement, comma rules.

Find the Direct Object

The direct object receives the action of the verb. Find it by asking "what?" after the verb. Only transitive verbs have direct objects — you cannot ask "what?" after an intransitive verb. Linking verbs (is, am, was, seem, become...) connect the subject to a complement, not a direct object. Tap the correct phrase from the options below each sentence.

Find the True Subject

The subject performs or is described by the verb. Find it by asking "who or what?" before the verb. The subject is never inside a prepositional phrase (phrases starting with along with, within, of, in, among...). In an inverted sentence the subject may follow the verb.
