Why does competing feel so good, and so bad?

A competition can make your heart race in the best way. Or the worst. Read, explore, and click to reveal.

Adrenaline Rush

Competition triggers a release of adrenaline, a hormone that boosts energy and focus in the short term. But if it never turns off, it becomes chronic stress.

🤝

Social Buffering

Working with others lowers your cortisol, the stress hormone. A trusted group literally calms your brain, freeing up working memory for learning.

📉

Eustress vs Distress

Eustress is positive stress: the kind that sharpens you. Distress is the kind that blocks thinking. The difference is often how safe you feel.

The Yerkes-Dodson Curve

First described by psychologists Robert Yerkes and John Dodson in 1908, this model shows the relationship between arousal, meaning your level of mental and physical activation, and performance. The key insight is that this relationship is not linear: it forms an inverted U-shape.

At very low levels of arousal, such as when you are bored, tired, or completely unstressed, performance is poor because you simply are not engaged enough to focus. As arousal increases, so does performance, up to a point. At moderate levels of stress or excitement, the brain is alert, focused, and working at its best. This is the zone of eustress.

Beyond that peak, however, additional arousal begins to hurt rather than help. When stress becomes too intense, driven by high stakes, time pressure, fear of failure, or public humiliation, the brain shifts into a threat-response mode. Cognitive resources that should be used for thinking and problem-solving are redirected toward managing the emotional response. Working memory shrinks, creativity drops, and performance collapses. Importantly, the width of the optimal zone varies between tasks: simple or well-practised tasks can tolerate higher arousal, while complex, creative, or unfamiliar tasks require calmer conditions to perform well.

The mental space you use to hold and process information right now. Worry and anxiety reduce it.

All the mental processes involved in learning, remembering, and solving problems.

Your level of alertness or activation, ranging from deep sleep to extreme excitement or panic.

Tab 2 · Activity

Black Hat or White Hat?

What actually makes us want to do something, and keep doing it? The answer matters more than you might think.

The terms Black Hat and White Hat come from Octalysis, a framework developed by game designer Yu-kai Chou to explain what drives human behaviour. Black Hat motivators, such as scarcity, urgency, social pressure, and the fear of loss, are powerful in the short term. They create a sense of obsession and can push you to act fast. But they come at a cost: over time, they leave you feeling anxious, controlled, and burned out, because they work by making you feel that something bad will happen if you don't act.

White Hat motivators, by contrast, such as curiosity, a sense of purpose, creative freedom, and belonging to a group, feel energising rather than draining. They tap into what psychologists call intrinsic motivation: doing something because it is genuinely meaningful or enjoyable, not because you are afraid of the consequences. Research consistently shows that White Hat motivation leads to deeper learning, better retention, and greater long-term wellbeing.

Neither type is simple, however: even White Hat motivators can be misused, and a small dose of Black Hat pressure (like a deadline) can sometimes help.
