Subject-Verb Agreement

B1–B2 Grammar 4 sections

Every verb in English has to match its subject in number. One dragon breathes — several dragons breathe. That matching is called subject-verb agreement, and breaking it is one of the most noticeable grammar errors in written English, even when the meaning is otherwise clear. The core rule is straightforward; the tricky part is finding the real subject when sentences get complicated.

Sentence visualiser
The dragon breathes  fire every night.
Error: "The dragons" is plural, but "breathes" is a singular verb form. The root form — breathe — is needed here.
Subject — who or what the sentence is about
Verb — must agree in number with the subject
The rule
Singular
One subject → verb adds -s or -es
When the subject is a single person, thing, or place (he, she, it, or a singular noun), the present-tense verb takes an -s or -es ending.
She walks to school.
The witch casts a spell.
It hisses in the dark.
Plural
Two or more subjects → verb stays in root form
When the subject refers to more than one person or thing (they, we, you, or a plural noun), the verb uses its base form — no ending added.
They walk to school.
The witches cast their spells.
The cats hiss at midnight.
When to add -s vs -es
The third-person singular ending rule
Add -es when the verb "hisses" — use -s for everything else.
Add -es
Verb ends in one of these sounds:
-ch -s -sh -x -z
watch→watches
miss→misses
push→pushes
fix→fixes
buzz→buzzes
Add -s
All other verbs:
walk→walks
eat→eats
sleep→sleeps
run→runs
think→thinks
Exception
Consonant + -y at the end → change to -ies:
fly→flies
carry→carries
study→studies
deny→denies

The core rule is simple when the subject is obvious. It gets harder when sentences use compound subjects, joining words like or and nor, or have extra phrases inserted between the subject and the verb. This section works through each scenario in order of difficulty.

1
Compound subjects with "and"
Two or more subjects joined by and almost always form a plural compound and need a plural verb. The exception is when the compound refers to a single fixed concept — a dish, a title, an established phrase. In those cases the verb stays singular.
A dragon and a phoenix are both mythical creatures. — two separate things → plural
Fish and chips is the national dish of England. — one fixed dish → singular
Law and order is the main theme of the novel. — single concept → singular

2
Or and nor — the proximity rule
When subjects are joined by or or nor (including either…or and neither…nor), the verb agrees with the subject nearest to it — not the first one listed. Swap the subjects and the verb must change too.
Neither the captain nor the sailors are ready. — sailors (pl) nearest → are
Neither the sailors nor the captain is ready. — captain (sg) nearest → is

3
"Of" phrases
The true subject always comes before a phrase beginning with of. The noun inside the "of" phrase describes the subject — it does not replace it. Strip the phrase out and the true subject becomes clear.
The pack of wolves howls at the moon. — pack (sg) governs the verb
The pages of the notebook were torn. — pages (pl) governs the verb

4
Interrupting phrases
Phrases like as well as, along with, together with, and in addition to can look as though they extend the subject — but they don't. They are parenthetical additions, usually set off by commas. Strip them out and the true subject, with its original number, remains.
The queen, as well as her guards, is attending. — queen (sg) governs the verb
The students, along with their tutor, are preparing. — students (pl) governs the verb

Three verbs cause most agreement errors in written English: to be, to do, and sentences with inverted word order. Each has irregular forms or structural traps that break the normal pattern. This section tackles all three in order of complexity.

1
To be — is, are, was, were
To be is the most irregular verb in English — it has distinct forms for almost every subject and tense. The most common errors are using was with a plural subject, or were with a singular subject in a factual statement.

am and is are unique forms — all other present-tense subjects use are. In the past, the split is simple: singular subjects (I, he, she, it) use was; plural subjects (you, we, they) use were.
Fact vs wish — the subjunctive
There is one important exception to the singular/plural split: hypothetical if-clauses and wishful statements. In these contexts, were is used for all subjects — including singular ones like I, he, and she. This is the subjunctive mood, and it signals that something is imagined, not real.
Fact
She was tired after the race.
A real past event. Singular subject → was.
Wish / hypothetical
If she were a champion, she would train differently.
Imagined, not real. All subjects → were, even singular ones.
The rule: In hypothetical if-clauses and wishful statements, use were for every subject — including I, he, she, and it. If the statement is factual, use the normal was / were split.

2
To do — does and do
To do follows the standard third-person singular rule: does for he, she, and it — do for everyone else. The same split applies in statements, questions, and negatives.
She does not agree.  ·  Does he know? — singular → does
They do not agree.  ·  Do you know? — plural / you / I / we → do

3
Inverted word order — here and there
In sentences beginning with here or there, the verb comes before the subject. The subject is hidden after the verb — and the verb must agree with it. The error is almost always defaulting to a singular verb before the subject appears. To find the true subject, flip the sentence into normal word order.
There are three wolves at the door. — three wolves are there → plural
Here is the key you lost. — the key is here → singular
