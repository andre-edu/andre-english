Research Report · Labour Market & The Future · May 2026

Jobs of the Future:
What Do We Actually Know?

An evidence-based look at global and Nordic trends — and a critical examination of the claim that "practical jobs" are better than graduate careers for the future.

Sources: WEF, McKinsey, OECD, SKR, Saco, Swedish Employment Agency, SCB, Stanford HAI, Goldman Sachs
Geography: Global + Sweden / Nordic focus
Time horizon: 2026–2040

Summary & Main Conclusion

People often say: "Get a practical job — that's the future." But this idea mixes up three separate questions: the risk of automation, the demand caused by population changes, and changes in pay and status. These three things do not always point in the same direction.

Data from the world's leading research organisations shows a more complex picture. Right now, AI mostly threatens routine office jobs and entry-level graduate roles — not advanced jobs that need creativity, ethical judgment, and complex human interaction. Physical jobs in unpredictable environments (plumbers, electricians) are relatively safe in the short term. But so are doctors, psychologists, and teachers.

Health and care services do need more workers — but because of population changes, not because university education is becoming less useful. The argument is actually for training more people in healthcare graduate roles (nurses, doctors, social workers), and for raising pay in the care sector.

Short version: The future job market rewards complexity, human contact, and the ability to adapt — whether you work with your hands or your mind. It is not practical vs. academic. It is routine vs. complex.

The most important WEF finding: human qualities are rising in value

The WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025 is based on surveys with over 1,000 employers, representing 14 million employees. It identifies the four fastest-growing skills for 2030: creative thinking, resilience (the ability to handle pressure and change), curiosity, and leadership. This is the biggest shift towards purely human skills that WEF has ever recorded in this report series.

"Leadership and social influence has seen the most substantial increase in importance, with a 22 percentage-point rise in the share of respondents identifying them as core skills — the largest shift we have ever recorded."

World Economic Forum, Future of Jobs Report 2025

McKinsey confirms this. Demand for social and emotional skills is expected to grow by up to 26% in the US and 22% in Europe by 2030. The OECD's Learning Compass 2030 framework is built around three core human abilities: creating new value, resolving dilemmas (difficult choices with no easy answer), and taking responsibility.

The Real Threat from AI — Who Is Actually Affected?

The most surprising and important finding from 2024–2025 research is that AI automation turns the traditional automation story upside down. Earlier waves of technology threatened blue-collar jobs — repetitive physical work. AI's current abilities are aimed at cognitive tasks: writing, analysis, coding, research. These are the core skills of office work.

"The Stanford HAI AI Index noted a marked increase in AI adoption in knowledge work sectors — finance, law, software, and professional services — and identified knowledge workers as disproportionately exposed compared to manual laborers. This flips the conventional automation narrative."

Stanford HAI AI Index 2024

The important distinction: tasks vs. jobs

Research consistently shows that AI replaces tasks, not whole jobs. A lawyer who currently spends 40% of their time reviewing documents can use AI to refocus on more complex advice. A doctor can use AI for routine diagnosis and spend more time on complex cases and patient communication. This means the number of people in a profession may fall without the profession disappearing — and the level of education required may actually increase.

Important finding: Gartner 2025
Fewer than 1% of global layoffs in 2025 could be directly linked to AI productivity gains. Most companies are using AI as a strategic narrative to justify traditional cost-cutting.

In other words: the "white-collar recession" of 2024–2025 is caused by the economic cycle, rising interest rates, and pandemic over-hiring — not because AI has actually taken the jobs yet. But this does not change the long-term direction.

Sweden and the Nordic Countries: The Power of Demographics

In Sweden and the Nordic countries, there is one extra factor that shapes the picture: demographics (population change). The ageing of the population creates a structural shortage of workers that is independent of AI cycles and the economy — and it is this shortage that is often misunderstood in the debate about "practical jobs".

The big structural problem

SKR — the Association of Swedish Municipalities and Regions — identifies one of Sweden's most serious structural challenges. The welfare sector needs 410,000 more employees by 2031. But only 253,000 new people of working age will enter the labour market during the same period. The gap is almost 160,000 people. This cannot be solved by recruitment alone. It is a mathematical problem, not simply a question of pay or attractiveness.

Critically: almost all of the growth in the working-age population consists of people born abroad. Today, more than one in three care assistants in municipalities are foreign-born, as are almost half of dentists and more than one in three doctors in regional healthcare.

The Swedish Employment Agency's skills analysis for 2030

The Swedish Public Employment Agency (Arbetsförmedlingen), working with McKinsey, analysed how skills needs are changing on the Swedish labour market up to 2030. Key findings:

  • → Social and empathy skills are expected to grow as a share of working hours from 21% to 25% by 2030 — an increase in every industry.
  • → Digital and tech skills are growing, but it is mainly basic digital knowledge — not advanced programming — that drives this. This benefits a wide range of workers, not just tech specialists.
  • → Manual and physical skills are falling the most in relative demand, but remain the largest single category in absolute terms in 2030. They are not disappearing.

Nordic comparison to 2035

A Nordic Council study (2024) on lifelong learning finds that all five Nordic countries face similar skills needs to 2035. These are driven by three major trends: an ageing population, digitalisation, and the green energy transition. In all five countries, the greatest needs are in healthcare, technology / IT, education, and green energy.

04

Examining the Claim

Claim being examined
"Get a practical job like elder care or construction — graduate careers will be less relevant in the future."
Partly true
Misleading simplification
Misses the real point

What is true in the claim

1. Healthcare and care services need many more workers. This is unavoidable based on population data. SKR's forecast is clear: elder care needs 30% more staff by 2031. This is a real argument for more people training in healthcare and care.

2. Some graduate careers have a weak job market. Generalist communications professionals, cultural heritage specialists, and broad social science graduates without a clear occupational identity do face genuine difficulties. AI tools are compressing parts of their core tasks (text production, research), and the job market is already saturated in some of these areas.

3. Hands-on trades are relatively safe in the short term. Goldman Sachs, McKinsey and WEF agree: physical jobs in unpredictable environments — plumbers, electricians, heating engineers, service technicians — are hard to automate with current robotics and AI. In the short term, this provides stability.

What is false or misleading in the claim

1. "Healthcare and care" is not an argument against university education. The roles in healthcare with the greatest demand and best job market are strongly academic: doctors, nurses (3-year university degree), midwives (master's level), psychologists, social workers, physiotherapists. Care assistants are also needed, of course — but they generally earn less, work in more difficult conditions, and have fewer career options.

2. Construction is not "safe" in the long term. McKinsey estimates that machine operation in construction (diggers, cranes) has 88% automation potential. Autonomous heavy equipment already exists commercially. Bricklaying robots are being developed. It is true that a carpenter solving unpredictable problems on a building site is hard to replace — but this is neither a simple nor a comfortable job.

3. AI is currently threatening entry-level knowledge work most, not advanced graduate roles. An experienced doctor, a senior lawyer, or a specialist teacher does not compete with AI — they are enhanced by it. It is the junior analyst, the entry-level programmer, and the generalist communicator who face pressure from substitution.

4. The claim confuses relative and absolute growth. If AI specialists grow by 110% in percentage terms and care assistants "only" grow by 30%, this says nothing about which sector creates more jobs in total. In absolute numbers, healthcare, schools, and social care will still create far more jobs.

What the claim misses: the real dividing line

The most important distinction in the research is not practical vs. university education. It is:

Vulnerable category Robust category
Routine tasks (regardless of collar colour) Complex, context-dependent tasks
Structured environments (the same tasks every day) Unstructured environments (every situation is different)
Jobs with low human-to-human interaction Jobs with high empathetic and social components
Entry-level roles without specialisation Deep specialist expertise in a specific field
Generalism with no clear market value Clear occupational identity with market value

A care assistant with 20 years of experience in dementia care and an experienced construction project manager are both robust in the job market. But they compete in completely different sectors and have very different pay trajectories. Treating them as equally "the future" and contrasting them with "all graduate careers" is not meaningful as an analysis.

05

Three Levels of Certainty

✓ What we know with high confidence

  • Population ageing in Sweden and the Nordic countries creates a structural shortage of healthcare and care workers that is impossible to avoid — no matter what AI does. SKR's forecasts are solid.
  • AI and automation will create more net jobs globally by 2030, not fewer. WEF's analysis of 1,000+ employers shows a net growth of +78 million jobs.
  • Manual and physical skills are declining in relative share but remain the largest category in absolute numbers. They are not disappearing.
  • AI is currently targeting routine, structured cognitive tasks — not whole jobs. It is tasks, not professions, that are being automated.
  • The four fastest-growing skills are genuinely human: creative thinking, resilience, curiosity, leadership. (WEF 2025)
  • Social and empathy skills are growing in demand across all industries in Sweden by 2030. (Swedish Employment Agency / McKinsey)
  • In 6 out of 10 Swedish municipalities, the working-age population is shrinking — this creates regional problems that are independent of national trends.

~ What we probably know

  • Entry-level office jobs will continue to fall in percentage terms over the next 5–10 years, though this is more due to "growth without hiring" strategies than mass replacement by AI.
  • Jobs requiring deep specialisation, ethical judgment, and complex human interaction (doctors, therapists, experienced teachers, managers) will remain robust and in demand.
  • Generalist roles without a clear occupational identity — broad communications, general social science, cultural roles without a specific market — will remain weak in the job market.
  • The green energy transition will create new demand for technical roles: solar panel technicians, wind turbine engineers, energy optimisation specialists, sustainability analysts.
  • Nordic rearmament (defence spending increases) will create genuinely new jobs over the next 10 years: military officers, cybersecurity specialists, and engineers in the defence industry.
  • AI will enhance (strengthen) more jobs than it eliminates in the period 2025–2035 — but the distribution of these effects may be uneven and hit young adults hardest.
  • Lifelong learning and regular retraining (every 5–8 years) will become the norm rather than the exception for most working people.

? What we can speculate about

  • Whether humanoid robots (Tesla Optimus and others) will actually succeed at unstructured physical work at scale within 15–20 years. If they do, this would dramatically change trades and manual jobs. The timeline is extremely uncertain.
  • Whether AI agents will reach the level of replacing whole workflows rather than individual tasks. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei predicts that half of all entry-level office work could disappear within 5 years — but this is a maximum scenario, not a consensus view.
  • Whether Sweden will solve its care gap with technology (AI-assisted care, robots), immigration of workers, pay increases — or a combination. SKR is clear: recruitment alone is not enough.
  • Whether "human authenticity" — proving that something was created by a human — will create a pay and status premium in creative industries, as some research suggests.
  • Whether the 4-day working week will spread in the Nordic countries as a response to AI productivity gains, reducing the total hours needed without reducing employment.
  • How AI will affect the education sector: whether AI tutors can genuinely substitute for some teaching, and what this means for teachers beyond 2035.
  • Whether a universal basic income or similar policy reform will be introduced in a Nordic context as a response to structural displacement — still a fringe scenario, but now discussed seriously by policy researchers.
06

Conclusions and Implications

1. The question is framed in the wrong way

The debate about "practical jobs vs. graduate careers" dissolves when you realise that the most relevant distinction is a different one: routine vs. complex, and generalist with no market value vs. specialist with a clear occupational identity. A nurse, a teacher, and a civil engineer are all "graduate careers" — and all three have strong prospects. A generalist communications professional and a budget analyst are also "graduate careers" — and both face structural pressure from AI.

2. Healthcare and care as an argument is more complex than it sounds

It is true that healthcare and care needs enormous numbers of new workers. But the graduate roles in healthcare — nurses, midwives, social workers, psychologists — have the best job market prospects, better pay, and stronger career opportunities. Care assistants are also unavoidably needed, but this is not an argument against graduate education in general.

3. Construction is not a simple safe choice

Electricians and plumbers are robust in the short term. But machine operators and some construction roles have high automation potential over a 15–25 year horizon. "Get a practical job in construction" is advice with a use-by date that nobody can predict exactly.

4. Global consensus points to human skills — but combined with digital understanding

WEF, McKinsey, and OECD all agree that the skills rising most in value are creative thinking, leadership, empathy, and resilience — combined with basic digital literacy. This is an argument for broad university education with strong career links, not against it.

5. Lifelong learning is the actual solution

In a job market where 39% of core skills are expected to change by 2030 — and where defence spending increases, the green transition, and AI adoption are all driving change at the same time — the most important quality is not which education you start with. It is the ability to keep learning and adapting. This is what WEF calls "resilience and adaptability" — the single most important skill for the future.

The future of work belongs to those who can handle complexity, work with AI as a tool rather than seeing it as a competitor, and hold on to the genuinely human abilities that machines still cannot replicate: judgment, empathy, and contextual wisdom.

Summary of consensus: WEF 2025 · McKinsey 2024 · OECD 2024 · Swedish Employment Agency 2024 · Saco 2026
Appendix

The Skills Value of School Subjects

A common question is how secondary school subjects connect to the future job market. The short answer: they do — but not in the direct way that careers advice often suggests. Secondary school is not designed to prepare you for a specific job in the way that History leads to archiving and Maths leads to engineering. Something more fundamental is happening.

WEF, McKinsey, and OECD consistently talk about skills — not subject knowledge. The skills rising most in value by 2030 are analytical thinking, creative thinking, resilience, ethical judgment, and complex communication. School subjects train exactly these skills — when they are taught well. The table below shows the connection between subject, skill, and relevance, and highlights some non-obvious links.

Do not read this table as "this subject leads to these jobs". Read it as "this subject trains these abilities, and here is why those abilities are hard to automate and valuable in 2030+".

This is a profession that requires exactly the skills that Religious Studies and Philosophy train: holding moral dilemmas open without oversimplifying them.

What the subject actually trainsHandling effort, setbacks, and performance under pressure. Cooperating towards a shared goal. Understanding how the body and mental capacity are connected — and looking after your health as a long-term project.
Why it is valuable in 2030+WEF lists resilience and wellbeing as one of the most important qualities to 2030 — not as a soft bonus, but as productive capacity. In an era of rapid change and constant retraining, the ability to stay functional under pressure is a competitive advantage. Employers pay for it.
The honest connection — and its limits: PE is the subject with the weakest direct link to specific future jobs. But the link is still real: research shows that physical activity improves cognition, stress tolerance, and decision-making — all qualities that are highly valued. It is not a career path, it is a foundation for all other career paths.
Overall conclusion: The core subjects at secondary school train — in essence — the skills that WEF, McKinsey, and OECD identify as most valuable in 2030: analytical thinking, ethical judgment, communication, and resilience. The one genuine gap between what school delivers and what the job market demands is explicit digital and AI literacy.
